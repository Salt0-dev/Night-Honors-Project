Book Analysis: Night by Elie Wiesel

Summary

“Night” by Elie Wiesel is a memoir that describes his experience of the awful events that occurred during the holocaust. Elie Wiesel used many literary elements to help the reader visualize the horrors that the Jewish people experienced by the Nazis. Two of Wiesel's most significant literary elements were imagery and symbolism. Wiesel uses imagery to paint vivid pictures of the awful conditions he and others endured during the holocaust. For example, he describes the horrible sight and stench of the burning bodies in the crematorium and the terrible conditions and suffering the prisoners were put through. The picture this imagery paints is realistic and helps the reader understand the horrific things that occurred during the holocaust. Wiesel also uses symbolism in “Night”. Throughout the story, Wiesel uses many symbols, but one of the most important is when he says: “Never Shall I forget that night, the first night in camp, that turned my life into one long night seven times sealed” (Wiesel 34). This symbolizes the endless suffering endured by the prisoners of the camp. He also said: “Never shall I forget those moments that murdered my god and my soul and turned my dreams to ashes” (Wiesel 34). This symbolizes the loss of hope and faith throughout the story. The themes of “Night” are mostly still relevant in today's society. The story’s message is to not lose hope even in the worst conditions. This can be applied to the recent Covid-19 pandemic, where many people were quarantined inside their homes, unable to visit with friends and family. The story also stresses the theme of standing up for justice. This can especially be applied today as there are still examples of ongoing hatred and exploitation of human rights in our society. Overall, “Night” is a powerful story that uses imagery and symbolism to propel the themes and describe the awful experiences of the Jewish people during the holocaust. It should also serve as a reminder that these events should never happen again. Work Cited Wiesel, Elie. Night. Farrar, Straus and Giroux, 2006.

Imagery and Symbolism

Wiesel uses vivid imagery to describe the horrors of the Holocaust, such as the overcrowded and unsanitary conditions in the concentration camps, the constant hunger and thirst, and the ruthless treatment of the prisoners by the guards. This imagery helps to convey the reality of the Holocaust to the reader.

Additionally, Wiesel uses symbolism, such as the image of fire, to represent the destruction and violence of the Holocaust. The fire is first mentioned when Wiesel and his family are forced to leave their home and are taken to a concentration camp. Later in the story, Wiesel uses the image of fire to symbolize the violence and destruction that he witnesses in the camps, as well as the emotional and psychological toll that the Holocaust takes on him and other prisoners.

Themes

"Night" explores themes of justice, human rights, hatred, and the power of the human spirit. The story emphasizes the importance of standing up for justice and human rights, and the dangers of hatred and bigotry. It also serves as a reminder of the strength and resilience of the human spirit, and of the importance of never giving up in the face of adversity.
